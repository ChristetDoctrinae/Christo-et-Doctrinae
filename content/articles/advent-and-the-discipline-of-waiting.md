We have lost the art of waiting.

This is not simply a complaint about impatience or the shortening of attention spans, though those things are real. It is a deeper observation about the shape of contemporary life. We inhabit a world that has, as far as possible, eliminated the gap between desire and satisfaction. We want a thing; we have it. We need an answer; we find it. We want connection; we reach for our phones.

Into this world, the Church brings the strange gift of Advent.

## The Strangeness of Waiting

Advent is, at its core, an invitation to wait. This might seem odd, given that we already know what we are waiting for. We know the story. We know that Christmas is coming. What is there to wait for?

But this misunderstands the nature of liturgical time. The Church does not pretend that what has not happened historically has not happened. It does something more interesting: it inhabits the waiting of those who came before us, and in doing so, discovers that it is also our own waiting.

We are not pretending to wait for Christ's first coming. We are learning, through the posture of Advent, to wait for his second — and to wait well. The season teaches us that waiting is not simply the empty time between now and then. It is a spiritual practice, a way of being, a form of faithfulness.

## What Waiting Requires

To wait well requires several things.

It requires, first, *attention*. Waiting is not passive. The person who waits well is the person who keeps watch — who does not fall asleep, who does not become distracted by lesser things, who keeps the horizon in view. This is why Advent has always been associated with wakefulness. *Stay awake*, the Gospel says. *You do not know the day or the hour.*

It requires, second, *hope*. Hope is not the same as optimism. Optimism is a temperamental disposition — the conviction that things will probably work out. Hope is a theological virtue — the conviction that God will be faithful, even when the evidence is ambiguous. The difference matters. Optimism can be undermined by circumstances. Hope, rightly understood, cannot, because it does not rest on circumstances but on the character of God.

It requires, third, *desire*. This is perhaps the most countercultural thing Advent asks of us. We live in a world that has taught us to manage and satisfy desire, not to cultivate it. But Advent asks us to want something we do not yet have — to feel the ache of absence, the longing for what has not yet arrived.

The mystics understood that desire, rightly ordered, is not a weakness but a form of prayer. Augustine's restless heart, which does not find rest until it rests in God — this is not a deficiency to be overcome. It is the soul oriented correctly, toward its proper end.

## The Gift of Incompleteness

There is a moment in Advent — usually somewhere in the third or fourth week — when the beauty of the season becomes almost unbearable. The O Antiphons, the candles, the hymns that reach toward something just out of grasp. *O come, O come, Emmanuel.* The longing in that music is not incidental to its beauty; it *is* its beauty.

This is the gift of Advent: it gives us permission to be incomplete. In a world that demands that we be finished, sufficient, self-contained, the season of Advent says: you are not there yet. None of us is. And that is all right, because what we are waiting for is coming.

The darkness of December is not an obstacle to the celebration — it is the condition that makes the light meaningful. You cannot fully appreciate what you have never been without.

And so we wait. We light our candles against the dark. We sing our songs of longing. We hold the ache of absence alongside the confidence of hope. And we wait — not passively, but actively; not with resignation, but with expectation.

*Come, Lord Jesus.*
