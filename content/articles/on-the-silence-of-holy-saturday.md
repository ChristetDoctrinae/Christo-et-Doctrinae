Holy Saturday is the day the Church does not know what to do with.

Good Friday has its stark liturgy — the veneration of the cross, the reading of the Passion, the stripping of the altars. Easter has its fire and its alleluias. But Holy Saturday is simply silence. The tabernacle is empty. The church is bare. There is nothing to do but wait.

This waiting is not comfortable. It is the waiting of people who have seen death and have not yet seen what comes after it. It is the waiting of the disciples in that upper room, the doors locked for fear. It is the waiting of Mary Magdalene, who has watched where they laid him.

The early Church called this day the *Harrowing of Hell* — Christ descending into the realm of the dead, liberating those who had waited from the beginning of time. But this is a doctrine about what is happening elsewhere, in the unseen places. For those who remain above ground, there is only silence.

What does it mean to inhabit this silence faithfully?

Perhaps it means learning to live in the between — between the death of what we knew and the birth of what we cannot yet imagine. Holy Saturday is not a theological problem to be solved. It is a spiritual reality to be inhabited.

The mystics knew this well. John of the Cross wrote of the *noche oscura*, the dark night in which the soul loses all familiar consolations and waits, stripped bare, for God. This is not a failure of faith. It is, paradoxically, a moment of purification — the self being emptied so that it might receive what it could not have received otherwise.

Holy Saturday invites us into that emptiness.

We live, in some sense, in a permanent Holy Saturday. We are the people who know both Good Friday and Easter — who have heard the proclamation that death is defeated — and yet who continue to watch people die, to see suffering persist, to find that the alleluias do not always come easily. We know the end of the story, and yet we still inhabit the middle of it.

This is not cause for despair. It is, rather, the precise condition that makes hope possible. Hope, as Paul insists, is not the same as sight. It is the disposition of those who wait — and who wait *well*, actively, with expectation, with their eyes open.

Holy Saturday is the school of that hope. It teaches us to wait without demanding resolution on our own timetable. It teaches us that silence is not absence. It teaches us that the empty tomb has not yet been found.

We wait.
