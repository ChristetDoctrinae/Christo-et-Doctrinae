There is a temptation, when confronted with the beauty of the world, to feel guilty about it.

The world is broken. We know this — not abstractly, but viscerally, from the news, from the hospital waiting room, from the face of the person who has lost someone they loved. How can one attend to the beauty of a piece of music, or a painting, or the evening light on water, when there is so much suffering?

This is not a trivial question. It has been asked by people of serious moral and spiritual intelligence. But I want to suggest that it rests on a misunderstanding — not of the suffering, but of the beauty.

## The Goodness of Creation

The Christian tradition begins with a declaration: *It is good.* This is not a concession or a qualified judgment. It is the verdict of the Creator on what has been made. The world, in its original constitution, is a gift — not a neutral background against which human drama unfolds, but something that participates in the goodness of the one who made it.

This has aesthetic implications. If creation is good, then the beauty we encounter in it — the beauty of a face, of a landscape, of a piece of music, of a poem — is not a distraction from reality but a disclosure of it. Beauty is one of the ways creation tells the truth about itself, about its maker.

The tradition has sometimes been suspicious of beauty, fearing that it leads away from God. But a deeper reading of the tradition shows the opposite. Beauty, rightly attended to, does not lead us away from God — it leads us toward God, because it is a reflection of divine beauty. *Omnis pulchritudo a te*, Augustine wrote. All beauty is from you.

## The Reality of Suffering

And yet: the world is also broken. This is not something the Christian tradition glosses over. The cross is at the center of the faith, and the cross is not beautiful in any straightforward sense. It is an instrument of torture. It represents the suffering of the innocent, the failure of justice, the apparent triumph of violence.

How does one hold together the goodness of creation and the reality of the cross?

This is where Christian aesthetics becomes genuinely difficult — and genuinely interesting. The answer is not to sentimentalize the suffering, turning the cross into a kind of decorative object drained of its horror. Nor is it to aestheticize suffering in a way that romanticizes it. The suffering is real. The horror is real.

But the Christian claim is that even this — even the cross — has been taken up into the redemptive work of God. The suffering is not negated; it is transfigured. And the beauty that emerges from this transfiguration is not cheap beauty. It is beauty that has passed through darkness and come out the other side.

Flannery O'Connor understood this. Her stories are full of violence, grotesquerie, and shock — and yet they are, finally, stories about grace. The violence is not decorative. It is the means by which her characters encounter the reality they have been evading. The beauty of her work is inseparable from its darkness.

## Learning to See

What does this mean for how we attend to beauty?

It means, first, that we can receive it without guilt. Beauty is not an escape from reality — it is a mode of encountering reality. When we attend to the beauty of a piece of music, we are not turning away from the broken world; we are receiving one of the gifts that the broken world still, despite everything, offers.

It means, second, that we should look for beauty in unexpected places. The Christian tradition has always been drawn to *kenotic* beauty — the beauty of self-emptying, of sacrifice, of the ordinary made luminous by attention. The beauty of a mother caring for a sick child is not less beautiful than the beauty of a sunset; it is, in many ways, more beautiful, because it has more weight.

And it means, finally, that the beauty we receive in this life is not the end of the story. The tradition speaks of a beauty that is coming — the restoration of all things, the city whose walls are jasper and whose streets are gold, the face of God seen directly. This is not escapism. It is the orientation that makes it possible to hold both the goodness and the brokenness of the world without being destroyed by either.

We are not required to choose between them. We are invited to hold them both, and to wait.
